A readiness survey translates the Readiness Assessment dimensions into measurable, respondent-friendly questions. Well-designed surveys surface honest assessment of organizational readiness without triggering defensive responses.

<Callout type="concept" title="Design for Honesty">
Readiness surveys often produce inflated scores because respondents don't want to appear unprepared. Design questions that make honest answers feel safe: use anonymity, frame questions as describing the current situation rather than rating individuals, and avoid leading language that signals the "right" answer.
</Callout>

## Survey Design Principles

- **Anonymous by default** — Respondents are more honest when they believe their identity is protected
- **Behavioral anchors** — Each scale point describes observable behaviors, not abstract quality levels
- **Scenario-based questions** — "When X happens, what does your organization typically do?" surfaces real practice rather than ideal practice
- **Short and focused** — 15–25 questions maximum; longer surveys produce lower completion rates and lower quality responses
