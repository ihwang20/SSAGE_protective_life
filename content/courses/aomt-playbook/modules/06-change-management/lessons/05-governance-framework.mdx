Governance provides the decision-making structures and accountability mechanisms that keep the change on track. A well-designed governance framework balances oversight with agility — enabling Pods to make decisions quickly while ensuring strategic alignment and risk management.

<Callout type="concept" title="Governance Enables Speed">
Well-designed governance makes decisions *faster*, not slower. The test: if a Pod can't ship this week because it's waiting for a governance decision, the governance model is broken. Clear decision rights, documented upfront, eliminate the need for consensus on routine decisions. Escalation paths are for genuine strategic conflicts — not routine approvals.
</Callout>

## Pod Decision Rights (What Pods Own)

Pods should be empowered to make the following decisions autonomously, without requiring governance approval:

| Decision Type | Pod Owns | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Backlog prioritization | Pod decides | PM owns the backlog; WSJF informs sequencing |
| Sprint scope | Pod decides | Within committed outcome and quarterly objective |
| Solution approach | Pod decides | Tech Lead makes architecture calls within defined constraints |
| User research methods | Pod decides | PM selects discovery approach |
| Model tuning and retraining | Pod decides | Within approved model risk guidelines |
| New data source access | Escalate if PII or regulated data | Data governance review required for sensitive sources |
| Cross-Pod dependencies | Cross-Pod sync | Lightweight weekly sync; no committee needed |
| Scope expansion beyond original outcome | Escalate to Steering | Budget or strategic direction change required |
| Model deployment in production | Escalate: Model Risk + Compliance | Insurance regulatory requirements apply |

## Insurance-Specific Governance Considerations

<Callout type="tip" title="Model Risk and Explainability">
Insurance regulators increasingly require that AI decisions affecting policyholders be explainable and auditable. Build explainability requirements into model acceptance criteria from the start — not as a post-deployment remediation. Governance should include a standing practice of model cards: structured documentation of what a model does, what data it was trained on, known limitations, and monitoring commitments.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning" title="Regulatory Variation by Line of Business">
State insurance regulators have different requirements for algorithmic decision-making across personal lines, commercial lines, and life/annuity products. Engage compliance early to map regulatory requirements by line of business before AI deployments touch customer-facing decisions. This is not optional — it is a deployment prerequisite for insurance AI.
</Callout>
