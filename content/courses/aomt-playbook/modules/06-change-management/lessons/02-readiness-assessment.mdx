The Readiness Assessment builds on the baseline by evaluating three additional dimensions that determine how the organization will respond to the specific changes being proposed. While the baseline measures general change capacity, the assessment evaluates readiness for this particular change.

## Dimension 1: Case for Change

The Case for Change answers the fundamental question: "Why must we change, and why now?" A compelling case requires:

- **Data-driven urgency** — Quantified evidence of the current-state gap
- **Stakeholder relevance** — Clear articulation of what the change means for each stakeholder group personally
- **Consequence of inaction** — A credible description of what happens if the organization does not change
- **Vision of success** — A tangible picture of what the improved future state looks and feels like

<Callout type="concept" title="Grounded in Diagnostic Findings">
The Case for Change is uniquely powerful because it is built on validated, quantified diagnostic findings. When you can say "our FCR is 62% versus a top-quartile benchmark of 85%, costing $2.4M annually in excess truck rolls," the urgency is self-evident.
</Callout>

## Dimension 2: Influence Network

The Influence Network maps the informal power structures that will accelerate or impede adoption. Three types of influencers matter:

<ProcessFlow steps={[
  { title: "Champions", description: "Enthusiastic supporters who actively promote the change to their peers. They provide social proof that the change is worthwhile and help overcome skepticism." },
  { title: "Skeptics", description: "Thoughtful critics who raise legitimate concerns. Engaging skeptics early strengthens the change plan and converts potential resistors into informed advocates." },
  { title: "Connectors", description: "People with broad social networks who amplify messages — positive or negative — across the organization. Their influence is disproportionate regardless of their personal opinion." }
]} />

<Callout type="tip" title="Identify the Top 5 Informal Leaders">
In every organization, a small number of individuals have disproportionate informal influence. They are not necessarily managers. Identify the top 5 informal leaders in the affected population, engage them early, and invest time in earning their support. Their endorsement is worth more than any formal communication campaign.
</Callout>
