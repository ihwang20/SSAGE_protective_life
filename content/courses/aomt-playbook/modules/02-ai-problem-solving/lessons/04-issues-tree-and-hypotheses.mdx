An issues tree is a structured decomposition of a problem into its potential root causes. It applies the MECE principle (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) to ensure that the full problem space is covered without overlap.

<Callout type="concept" title="MECE Principle">
Mutually Exclusive means no two branches overlap — the same root cause doesn't appear in multiple places. Collectively Exhaustive means all possible causes are covered — if the root cause exists, it must appear somewhere in the tree. AI excels at generating MECE decompositions quickly, but human domain expertise is required to validate completeness and relevance.
</Callout>

AI-generated decompositions are a starting point. Check each branch for genuine mutual exclusivity — is there overlap between branches? Validate with domain experts to catch blind spots the AI may miss.
</Callout>

## Hypothesis Framework

For each branch of the Issues Tree, formulate a Null Hypothesis (H0) and an Alternative Hypothesis (H1):

- **H0 (Null Hypothesis):** The default assumption that no significant relationship or difference exists. Example: "Dispatch routing method has no statistically significant effect on first-visit resolution rate."
- **H1 (Alternative Hypothesis):** The assertion that a meaningful relationship or difference does exist. Example: "AI-optimized dispatch routing improves first-visit resolution rate by at least 10 percentage points."

**Assumption-Driven (avoid this):** "We think the dispatch process is broken because technicians complain about it. Let's redesign the routing system."

**Hypothesis-Driven (do this instead):** "H0: Current dispatch routing has no significant effect on first-visit resolution. H1: Geographic clustering in dispatch reduces travel time by >15%. We will test with 6 months of dispatch data against resolution outcomes."

<Callout type="warning" title="Rejecting H0 Does Not Prove Causation">
Rejecting the null hypothesis demonstrates a statistically significant relationship — it does not prove that one variable causes the other. Correlation findings must be supplemented with qualitative validation, domain expertise, and where possible, controlled testing before claiming causation.
</Callout>

## Building the Tree Iteratively

Start with 3–5 top-level branches and expand as data reveals new dimensions. AI can suggest additional branches based on patterns it finds in the data, but the practitioner must validate each addition against the MECE framework to prevent overlap or gaps.
