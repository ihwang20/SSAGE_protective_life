AI solutions are inherently experimental. Requirements evolve as the solution is built and tested. Stakeholder understanding of what's possible changes with each working demo. Agile delivery is not just a preference — it is the only approach that works well for AI development.

<Callout type="concept" title="Why Agile for AI">
Traditional waterfall planning requires stable, knowable requirements. AI solution requirements are neither stable nor fully knowable at the start. The model's actual performance will differ from the proof of concept. User interaction patterns will differ from what was assumed. Agile delivery's short cycles and continuous feedback loops are specifically designed for this uncertainty.
</Callout>

## The Pod as the Unit of Delivery

All delivery work is organized into **Pods** — small, empowered, cross-functional teams that own a problem space end-to-end. A Pod typically includes a Product Manager, an engineer or data scientist, a designer or analyst, and a domain subject matter expert. Pods are stable over time; work comes to the Pod, not the other way around.

## When to Add Coordination (Not a Framework)

<Callout type="tip" title="Right-Size Your Coordination">
When a single Pod can't deliver the outcome alone — because the scope spans multiple systems, teams, or domains — add the minimum coordination mechanism needed: a shared backlog review, a weekly cross-Pod sync, or a lightweight dependency board. Add coordination incrementally as genuine blockers emerge. **Do not pre-install a multi-layer framework before you know you need it.**
</Callout>

| Situation | Appropriate Coordination | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| 1 Pod, 1 domain | Basic Scrum (sprint + demo + retro) | PI planning, ARB, Program Office |
| 2–3 Pods, shared dependencies | Weekly cross-Pod sync, shared dependency board | SAFe Agile Release Train ceremony stack |
| 4+ Pods, multiple business domains | Lightweight portfolio review (WSJF), async dependency mapping | Scaled framework pre-installation without evidence of need |
