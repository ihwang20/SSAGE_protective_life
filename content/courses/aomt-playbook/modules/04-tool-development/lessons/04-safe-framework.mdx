## The SVPG Product Model: Core Distinction

The fundamental shift in SVPG-style product operating models is **from feature factories to outcome owners**. In a feature factory, leadership defines what to build and teams execute. In an empowered product model, leadership defines the problem to solve and the outcome to achieve — and the Pod discovers the best solution through rapid experimentation.

<Callout type="concept" title="The Marty Cagan Critique of SAFe (Why It Matters Here)">
SAFe recreates waterfall at scale. PI Planning pre-defines 10 weeks of work before Pods have had the chance to discover whether those solutions are actually right. The Program Increment becomes a commitment to a specific solution, not a commitment to an outcome. For AI initiatives — where model behavior is uncertain and user adoption patterns are unknown — pre-committing to features 10 weeks out is precisely the wrong approach. **Commit to outcomes. Discover solutions.**
</Callout>

## The Empowered Product Manager Role

WSJF and opportunity documentation from the diagnostic phase inform the Product Manager — but the Product Manager's job is not simply to maintain a backlog. They are responsible for continuous discovery and validated learning:

<ProcessFlow steps={[
  { title: "Own the Outcome, Not the Feature List", description: "The PM is accountable for a measurable business outcome (e.g., +15 points FCR improvement within 2 quarters), not for delivering a specific set of features. How the outcome is achieved is discovered through iteration — not defined upfront." },
  { title: "Continuous Discovery", description: "Run at least 2–3 user interviews per week throughout delivery. Test assumptions with prototypes before building. Separate \"what do users need\" discovery from \"how should we build it\" delivery — both run continuously in parallel." },
  { title: "Prioritize with Evidence", description: "WSJF scoring from the diagnostic phase provides a starting prioritization signal. The PM updates the backlog as new evidence emerges from discovery and production metrics. Priorities are dynamic, not locked in a PI plan." },
  { title: "Define Success Before Building", description: "Every significant backlog item has a measurable hypothesis: \"We believe that [change] will result in [outcome] as evidenced by [metric].\" This is evaluated at the sprint review — not just \"did we ship?\" but \"did it work?\"" }
]} />

## Pod Structure for AI Delivery

| Role | Responsibility | Insurance Context |
|---|---|---|
| **Product Manager** | Owns outcome; runs discovery; maintains prioritized backlog | Bridges diagnostic findings to delivery; engages underwriting/claims SMEs continuously |
| **AI/Data Engineer** | Builds and maintains AI models and data pipelines | Manages insurance data quality constraints; ensures model auditability for compliance |
| **Designer / Analyst** | Translates discovery insights into testable prototypes | Designs for claims adjusters, underwriters, or operations staff — not technical users |
| **Domain SME** | Embedded expert on insurance processes and constraints | Validates that AI outputs are interpretable and usable within regulatory bounds |
| **Tech Lead** | Technical decisions within the Pod; API and integration ownership | Navigates legacy system constraints common in mid-size carriers |

## +AI to AI+ Maturity Path

For an insurance organization starting from a **+AI posture** (AI augmenting existing processes), the Pod model enables a disciplined maturity path toward **AI+ posture** (AI as a core operating capability in select domains) without requiring enterprise-wide transformation before any value is realized.

<Callout type="tip" title="Sequence Your Pod Investments">
**Start +AI:** Launch 1–2 assistive AI Pods in highest-value, highest-readiness domains (e.g., claims triage, underwriting data prep). Demonstrate outcome. Build organizational confidence and data infrastructure.

**Then AI+:** Once a Pod has demonstrated 2–3 successful outcome cycles, the domain has earned investment in more autonomous AI capability. The organizational muscle for continuous discovery and rapid iteration is now proven — expand from there.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning" title="What to Avoid in Year 1">
Do not install an enterprise-wide Agile Release Train, a central AI Center of Excellence with intake governance, or a Program Increment planning cadence before any Pod has shipped a working AI solution. These structures require organizational maturity that doesn't yet exist and will create the bureaucratic drag that kills AI momentum in insurance organizations.
</Callout>
